## Background

Angela Klein is a senior KDS behavioral scientist responsible for the psychological evaluation, training protocols, and quality assurance of operatives in the Legion program. Her work sits at the intersection of Project RISE -- KDS's classified cloning initiative -- and the day-to-day management of operative deployment at Garganta. She holds an A-prefix employee ID indicating executive-tier access, and her correspondence reveals a person who treats the manufacturing and behavioral programming of human beings with the same technical precision a quality engineer might apply to industrial output.

Klein's administrative duties span the full lifecycle of operative management. She issues field promotions, ensures Personnel Record Demographic Materials (PRDMs) are uploaded before shifts, and coordinates objective stacks for deployed quads. Her correspondence with Christopher Getz about promotions -- privates to corporals, corporals to sergeants -- reads as routine human resources paperwork, except that the "humans" in question are memory-wiped prisoners fitted with neural control chips.

Her more significant contributions concern the Sigma generation of Rise operatives and their unexpected behavioral development. Klein documented a remarkable incident in which a Sigma operative, during a routine mission, improvised a tactical use for PolyFoam -- a construction material the operative should have had no knowledge of. The operative sealed a security door with the foam during a retreat, demonstrating both retained memory across hydrostasis periods and creative problem-solving. When a different Sigma operative from a separate quad independently replicated the same behavior, Klein recognized the implications: the operatives were somehow communicating learned behaviors between themselves, potentially through BIOCOM, without authorization. Her report to Ellis Carnegie framed this as an urgent matter requiring investigation.

Klein also challenged the accelerated production timelines imposed on Project RISE. In a letter to Mr. Giraud, she argued that the 60-day maturity cycle produced "seeds" that were physically adult but psychologically juvenile -- "sentience is not a resilient enough foundation for us to build on." She advocated for longer development periods, better chemical supplementation, and a fundamental reevaluation of whether the program was sacrificing quality for speed. Her concern was not ethical in the humanitarian sense; it was about product reliability. She wanted functional, stable operatives, not disposable ones that broke under stress.

Separately, Klein investigated whether biological factors -- specifically pathogens surviving hydrostasis -- could be affecting operative behavioral assessment results. When Sebastian Hutcherson dismissed the possibility, insisting that "nothing survives HS unless we want it to," Klein pushed back: "There are things you don't know that you don't know." This exchange takes on darker resonance given that the NAM-V virus would ultimately prove capable of surviving conditions no one at Garganta anticipated.
