## Background

Ellis Carnegie is a specialist within Kovac Research's cybernetics division, working from the organization's Pretoria facility. He is the technical architect behind the behavioral encoding systems used to program Kovac Security Operators, and a key figure in the transition from reformed-prisoner operatives to the manufactured "Rise" clones that KDS intended to use as an infinitely renewable military workforce.

Carnegie's expertise lies in the Sec-B-M behavioral modification chips -- the neural implants installed in every KSO that allow BIOCOM to issue commands, track operatives, and suppress autonomous behavior. The chips represent the technological backbone of the entire Legion program: without them, the memory-wiped prisoners stored in hydrostasis would be confused, aggressive, and uncontrollable. With them, they become the obedient mercenary force that Kovac sells to its clients. Carnegie's work on chip specifications, upload protocols, and behavioral classification criteria is what makes the system function.

His most detailed surviving log is a letter to Anders Johanson analyzing the psychological dynamics of operative quads. Carnegie observed that operatives psychologically encoded as "Genesis" -- those with enhanced 5-HT (serotonin) receptor configurations -- naturally assumed leadership roles within minutes of deployment. However, he noted that operatives classified as "Faulty" sometimes demonstrated effective leadership under extreme duress, leading him to question whether KDS's classification standards were too rigid. Rather than discarding aberrant operatives, Carnegie argued, the program should develop corrective curricula to harness behavioral deviations rather than treating them as defects.

Carnegie's analysis of the transition to Rise operatives is particularly revealing. He acknowledged that manufactured soldiers would resolve many behavioral issues caused by social environment and prolonged hydrostasis, but warned that human nature itself posed irreducible challenges. "As a species, we have a propensity for violence," he wrote. "We divide into tribal groups that are political and encourage leadership competition." He predicted that even genetically manufactured operatives would exhibit alpha-beta dominance conflicts within quads, and that without a corrective behavior program, the result would be "excessive product waste." His language throughout -- "product," "trash heap," "acquisition," "genetic pool to grow from" -- reveals a worldview in which human beings are engineering materials to be optimized.

Carnegie operated primarily from outside Garganta, communicating with on-site personnel like Angela Klein and Anders Johanson through encrypted D-Lock channels. When Klein documented the Sigma generation's unexpected autonomous behaviors -- operatives improvising tools, retaining knowledge across hydrostasis, and potentially communicating learned behaviors to other quads -- she requested Carnegie visit Garganta in person to observe. Whether he made the trip is not recorded.

## Arc

- **September 2050**: Writes to Anders Johanson analyzing operative quad dynamics. Observes Genesis-type leadership emergence, questions "Faulty" classification criteria, and recommends corrective behavior programs for both current and future Rise operatives.
- **2051-2054**: Operates from Kovac Research's Pretoria facility, contributing to Sec-B-M chip development. The 120,000-unit chip order for Garganta (1300MB/s upload speed, version 2.7.4) reflects the scale of infrastructure his designs support.
- **August 2054**: Receives Angela Klein's urgent report on Sigma generation autonomous behavior. Klein requests his in-person presence at Garganta to investigate cross-operative behavior transmission.
- **Post-2054**: No further log appearances. The autonomous behaviors he was asked to investigate ultimately foreshadowed the breakdown of operative controllability that contributed to the facility's collapse.

## Key Relationships

- **Anders Johanson (A029)** -- KDS senior leader to whom Carnegie reported on operative behavioral encoding. Johanson was the strategic decision-maker; Carnegie provided the technical foundations.
- **Angela Klein (A106)** -- KDS behavioral scientist at Garganta who served as Carnegie's on-site counterpart. Klein sent him reports on operative anomalies and requested his physical presence to investigate Sigma generation autonomy.
- **BIOCOM** -- The facility AI system that Carnegie's chip designs interface with. BIOCOM executes the behavioral programs Carnegie develops, distributing commands and tracking operative status through the Sec-B-M implants.
