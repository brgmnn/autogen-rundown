## Background

Michel LeFavre served as Medical Director at Garganta, overseeing the medical department during the critical period of NAM-V's emergence within the facility. His position placed him at the intersection of rising psychological disturbances among workers and the corporate imperative to maintain operational readiness.

LeFavre's management style was characterized by a condescending, authoritative tone. He treated employee compliance as a matter of vocabulary comprehension rather than addressing the underlying reasons workers avoided their psychiatric evaluations. His memos carried the weight of institutional authority while revealing a disconnect from the human reality of life inside Garganta.

As Medical Director, LeFavre was responsible for mandating the psychiatric evaluations that Dr. August Teale conducted -- the same evaluations that documented the facility's accelerating psychological collapse. Whether LeFavre understood the true scope of what his medical staff was uncovering, or whether he simply processed the reports as administrative throughput, remains unclear.

## Arc

LeFavre's appearances are limited but telling. By December 2052, when he issued his facility-wide memo demanding attendance at mandatory psychiatric evaluations, the NAM-V outbreak was already well underway. The suicide rate was spiking, 34.4% of the workforce reported psychological disturbances, and workers were beginning to use forbidden slang like "Garganta Flu" and "The Stoops" to describe what was happening to them.

His response to this crisis was to issue a memo that included the dictionary definition of the word "mandatory." This act -- pedantic, tone-deaf, bureaucratically precise -- encapsulates the institutional failure that allowed the situation at Garganta to spiral beyond recovery. The medical system was not broken because workers misunderstood the word "mandatory." It was broken because there was nothing the medical system could actually do.
